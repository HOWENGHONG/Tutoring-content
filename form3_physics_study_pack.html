Section A — Multiple Choice  (10 marks, 1 mark each)

Ch.1 Q1
A car travels 100 m in 25 s at constant speed. What is its speed?
A. 0.25 m/s     B. 2.5 m/s     C. 4 m/s     D. 25 m/s
✅ C. 4 m/s
Speed = distance ÷ time = 100 ÷ 25 = 4 m/s
Ch.1 Q2
On a distance-time graph, a horizontal (flat) line means the object is:
A. Moving at constant speed     B. Accelerating     C. Stationary     D. Decelerating
✅ C. Stationary
A flat line means no change in distance over time — the object is not moving.
Ch.10 Q3
Sound waves are an example of:
A. Transverse waves     B. Longitudinal waves     C. Electromagnetic waves     D. Light waves
✅ B. Longitudinal waves
Sound particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel, forming compressions and rarefactions.
Ch.10 Q4
A wave has frequency 50 Hz and wavelength 2 m. What is its wave speed?
A. 25 m/s     B. 52 m/s     C. 100 m/s     D. 48 m/s
✅ C. 100 m/s
v = fλ = 50 × 2 = 100 m/s
Ch.11 Q5
Which type of electromagnetic wave has the HIGHEST frequency?
A. Radio waves     B. Infrared     C. X-rays     D. Gamma rays
✅ D. Gamma rays
Gamma rays have the highest frequency and shortest wavelength in the EM spectrum.
Ch.11 Q6
All electromagnetic waves share which property?
A. They require a medium to travel     B. They travel at 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum     C. They are longitudinal     D. They carry electric charge
✅ B. They travel at 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum
This is called the speed of light. All EM waves are also transverse and can travel through a vacuum.
Ch.12 Q7
A ray of light hits a mirror. The angle of incidence is 35°. What is the angle of reflection?
A. 55°     B. 35°     C. 70°     D. 90°
✅ B. 35°
Law of reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection. Both are measured from the normal (not the surface).
Ch.12 Q8
Total internal reflection can only occur when light travels from:
A. Less dense to more dense medium     B. More dense to less dense medium     C. Air to glass     D. Water to glass
✅ B. More dense to less dense medium
TIR occurs when light travels from dense → less dense (e.g. glass → air) AND the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle.
Ch.10 Q9
A wave has period T = 0.02 s. What is its frequency?
A. 0.02 Hz     B. 5 Hz     C. 50 Hz     D. 500 Hz
✅ C. 50 Hz
f = 1/T = 1 ÷ 0.02 = 50 Hz
Ch.11 Q10
Which EM wave is used in TV remote controls?
A. Microwaves     B. Ultraviolet     C. Infrared     D. X-rays
✅ C. Infrared
TV remotes emit infrared signals to communicate with the TV set.

Ch.1 Q11  ·  6 marks
A cyclist starts from rest and reaches 12 m/s in 8 seconds. She then travels at constant speed for 10 seconds, then brakes to a stop in 4 seconds.

(a) Calculate her acceleration in the first 8 s. (2 marks)
(b) Calculate the total distance travelled. (3 marks)
(c) On a v-t graph, describe the shape of each stage. (1 mark)
(a) a = (v − u) / t = (12 − 0) / 8 = 1.5 m/s²

(b)
Stage 1 (triangle): ½ × 8 × 12 = 48 m
Stage 2 (rectangle): 10 × 12 = 120 m
Stage 3 (triangle): ½ × 4 × 12 = 24 m
Total = 192 m

(c) Stage 1: straight line sloping UP to 12 m/s; Stage 2: horizontal line at 12 m/s; Stage 3: straight line sloping DOWN to 0.
Ch.10 Q12  ·  6 marks
A water wave has wavelength 3 m and travels at 6 m/s.

(a) Calculate the frequency of the wave. (2 marks)
(b) Calculate the period. (2 marks)
(c) State TWO differences between transverse and longitudinal waves. (2 marks)
(a) f = v / λ = 6 / 3 = 2 Hz

(b) T = 1 / f = 1 / 2 = 0.5 s

(c) Any two of:
• Transverse: vibrations are perpendicular to direction of travel. Longitudinal: vibrations are parallel.
• Examples: transverse = light / water waves; longitudinal = sound.
• Longitudinal waves have compressions and rarefactions; transverse have crests and troughs.

(a) List the 7 types of EM radiation in order from lowest to highest frequency. (3 marks)

(b) State ONE use and ONE danger for EACH of: ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays. (3 marks)
(a) Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible light → Ultraviolet → X-rays → Gamma rays

(b)

Ch.12 Q14  ·  6 marks
A ray of light travels from glass (denser) into air. The critical angle of glass is 42°.

(a) Describe what happens when the angle of incidence is exactly 42°. (1 mark)
(b) What happens when the angle of incidence is 50°? Name this phenomenon. (2 marks)
(c) State ONE application of this phenomenon. (1 mark)
(d) State TWO conditions needed for this to occur. (2 marks)
(a) The refracted ray travels along the boundary between the two media (angle of refraction = 90°).

(b) The ray is completely reflected back into the glass — this is Total Internal Reflection (TIR).

(c) Optical fibres / endoscopes / periscopes / binoculars / diamond cutting.

(d)
• Light must travel from a denser medium to a less dense medium (e.g. glass → air).
• The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle.
Ch.1 Q15  ·  6 marks
A distance-time graph shows the following:
• t = 0 to 5 s: object moves from 0 to 20 m
• t = 5 s to 10 s: object stays at 20 m
• t = 10 s to 14 s: object returns to 0 m

(a) What is the speed in the first 5 s? (2 marks)
(b) What is happening between t = 5 s and t = 10 s? (1 mark)
(c) What is the speed in the final stage (t = 10 to 14 s)? (2 marks)

(b) The object is stationary (not moving — flat line on d-t graph).

Ch.10 Q16  ·  4 marks
The diagram below shows a transverse wave. Label the following on the wave:
(a) the amplitude   (b) the wavelength   (c) a crest   (d) a trough

All formulas students must know and apply in exams. Learn the triangle method for rearranging.

Ch.1 — Movement and Position

Speed
v = d ÷ t
v = speed (m/s)  ·  d = distance (m)  ·  t = time (s)
Rearrange: d = v × t  ·  t = d ÷ v
Acceleration
a = (v − u) ÷ t
a = acceleration (m/s²)  ·  v = final velocity  ·  u = initial velocity  ·  t = time (s)
Deceleration = negative acceleration
Distance-Time Graph
gradient = speed
Steep slope = fast speed  ·  Flat line = stationary  ·  Curved line = changing speed (accelerating)
Gradient = rise ÷ run = Δd ÷ Δt
Velocity-Time Graph
gradient = acceleration
area = distance
Gradient (slope) = acceleration  ·  Area under graph = total distance
Use triangle area (½bh) or rectangle area (l×w)

Ch.10 — Properties of Waves

Wave Equation
v = f × λ
v = wave speed (m/s)  ·  f = frequency (Hz)  ·  λ = wavelength (m)
Rearrange: f = v ÷ λ  ·  λ = v ÷ f
Period and Frequency
T = 1 ÷ f
T = period (s) = time for ONE complete wave cycle
f = frequency (Hz) = number of waves per second
Also: f = 1 ÷ T

Ch.11 — Electromagnetic Spectrum

Speed of Light
c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
All EM waves travel at this speed in vacuum.
Use c instead of v when dealing with EM waves.
EM Wave Equation
c = f × λ
c = 3×10⁸ m/s  ·  f = frequency (Hz)  ·  λ = wavelength (m)
Higher frequency → shorter wavelength

Ch.12 — Light Waves

Law of Reflection
i = r
Angle of incidence = angle of reflection
⚠️ Both angles measured from the NORMAL (perpendicular to surface), NOT from the surface itself.
Snell's Law (Refraction)
n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
n = refractive index  ·  i = angle in medium 1  ·  r = angle in medium 2
Air / vacuum: n = 1.0
Critical Angle
sin C = 1 ÷ n
C = critical angle  ·  n = refractive index of the denser medium
When i > C → Total Internal Reflection occurs
⚠️ Common unit mistakes — check every answer!
• Frequency → unit is Hz (not m/s or m)
• Wavelength → unit is m (not Hz)
• Speed → unit is m/s
• Acceleration → unit is m/s²
• Always measure reflection/refraction angles from the NORMAL, not the mirror/surface
• Period (T) is in seconds, not Hz

Ch.1 — Movement and Position
Distance is the total length of path travelled (scalar — no direction). Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to finish, with direction (vector).
Speed = distance ÷ time. Velocity = speed with direction. They use the same formula but velocity includes a direction.
Acceleration = change in velocity ÷ time. Unit: m/s². Deceleration is negative acceleration (slowing down).
Distance-time graph: slope (gradient) = speed. Flat horizontal line = stationary. Upward straight line = constant speed. Steeper = faster. Curve (getting steeper) = accelerating.
Velocity-time graph: slope (gradient) = acceleration. Flat horizontal line = constant velocity. Area under the graph = total distance travelled.
To find area under v-t graph: use triangle formula (½ × base × height) for sloping sections, and rectangle formula (length × width) for flat sections.
Ch.10 — Properties of Waves
Transverse wave: particles vibrate PERPENDICULAR (at 90°) to the direction of wave travel. Examples: light, water waves, all EM waves.
Longitudinal wave: particles vibrate PARALLEL to the direction of wave travel. The wave creates compressions and rarefactions. Example: sound.
Amplitude: maximum displacement of a particle from its rest (equilibrium) position. Measured in metres. Greater amplitude = more energy carried by the wave.
Wavelength (λ): distance between two consecutive crests, or two consecutive troughs. Measured in metres (m).
Frequency (f): number of complete wave cycles per second. Measured in Hertz (Hz).
Period (T): time taken for one complete wave cycle. T = 1/f. Unit: seconds (s).
Wave equation: v = fλ. If frequency increases, wavelength decreases (speed stays the same in the same medium).
Diffraction: waves spread out when passing through a gap or around an obstacle. Most noticeable when the gap size ≈ wavelength.
Reflection: waves bounce off a surface. Refraction: waves change direction when they change speed (entering a different medium).
🧠 Memory trick — Transverse vs Longitudinal: "Trans = cross" — transverse vibrations cross (go perpendicular to) the direction of travel. Longitudinal = "long" — vibrations go along the direction of travel.
Ch.11 — The Electromagnetic Spectrum
Order from LOW to HIGH frequency (also SHORT to LONG wavelength if reversed): Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible light → Ultraviolet → X-rays → Gamma rays
All EM waves: (1) travel at speed of light (3×10⁸ m/s) in vacuum; (2) are transverse waves; (3) can travel through vacuum; (4) transfer energy; (5) obey v = fλ.
Radio waves: broadcasting (TV, radio, Wi-Fi). No significant danger at low intensities.
Microwaves: cooking (microwave ovens), satellite communication, mobile phones. Danger: can heat internal body tissues.
Infrared (IR): remote controls, thermal imaging cameras, night vision, heat lamps. Danger: skin burns.
Visible light: human vision, photography, optical fibres. The only EM waves our eyes can detect.
Ultraviolet (UV): sterilisation, detecting forged banknotes, sunbeds, fluorescent lights. Danger: skin cancer, cataracts (eye damage), sunburn.
X-rays: medical imaging (bones), airport security scanners. Danger: ionising radiation — causes cell and DNA damage, increased cancer risk. Lead aprons used for protection.
Gamma (γ) rays: cancer radiotherapy (kills tumour cells), sterilising medical/food equipment. Danger: most penetrating EM wave — ionising, causes DNA damage and cancer at high doses.

Ch.12 — Light Waves
Law of reflection: angle of incidence (i) = angle of reflection (r). BOTH angles are always measured from the NORMAL (a line perpendicular to the surface), NOT from the surface itself.
Refraction: light bends when it travels from one medium to another (different optical density). It bends TOWARDS the normal when entering a denser medium, and AWAY when leaving.
Refractive index (n): measures how much a medium slows light. Air = 1.0, glass ≈ 1.5, water ≈ 1.33.
Critical angle (C): the angle of incidence in the denser medium at which the refracted ray travels along the boundary (refraction angle = 90°). sin C = 1/n.
Total Internal Reflection (TIR): occurs ONLY when TWO conditions are met: (1) light travels from a denser medium to a less dense medium; AND (2) the angle of incidence is GREATER than the critical angle.
Applications of TIR: optical fibres (broadband internet, telephone), endoscopes (medical), periscopes, binoculars, diamond cuts (sparkling).
Convex (converging) lens: bends light rays inward to a focal point. Forms real, inverted image (for objects beyond focal point). Used in: cameras, projectors, human eye, magnifying glass (virtual image when object is close).
Concave (diverging) lens: bends light rays outward. Always forms virtual, upright, smaller image. Used in: spectacles for short-sighted people.
Dispersion: white light splits into 7 colours (ROYGBIV) when passing through a prism, because different colours refract by slightly different amounts. Red refracts least, Violet refracts most.
ROYGBIV order: Red · Orange · Yellow · Green · Blue · Indigo · Violet (Red at top, Violet at bottom of rainbow).
🧠 TIR — remember the TWO conditions (exam favourite!):
1. Dense → Less dense medium (e.g. glass → air, NOT air → glass)
2. Angle of incidence MUST be greater than the critical angle
If BOTH are true → TIR. If only one is true → refraction still occurs (light exits).
