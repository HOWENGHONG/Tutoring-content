Chapter 10 — Properties of Waves
Transverse & longitudinal · Amplitude · Wavelength · Frequency · Period · Wave equation v = fλ
📖 Concept 1 — Types of Waves
Transverse wave
Particles vibrate perpendicular (⊥) to direction of travel.
Has crests and troughs.
Examples: light, water waves, all EM waves.
Longitudinal wave
Particles vibrate parallel (∥) to direction of travel.
Has compressions and rarefactions.
Example: sound.
📐 Concept 2 — Wave Quantities & Formulas
Amplitude (A): maximum displacement from rest position → metres (m)
Wavelength (λ): distance from one crest to the next → metres (m)
Frequency (f): number of complete waves per second → Hertz (Hz)
Period (T): time for one complete wave → seconds (s)
Wave speed (v): how fast the wave moves → m/s
v = f × λ wave equation
T = 1 ÷ f period ↔ frequency
f = v ÷ λ find frequency
λ = v ÷ f find wavelength
⚠️ Common mistake: Amplitude ≠ crest-to-trough height. Amplitude is only half of that (rest position to crest).
EXAMPLE 1
Finding frequency and period from wave equation
Ch.10 Basics
A water wave travels at 12 m/s and has a wavelength of 4 m.
(a) Find the frequency.  (b) Find the period.
✏️ SOLUTION — Step by Step
1
Write down what you know:
v = 12 m/s  ·  λ = 4 m  ·  f = ?  ·  T = ?
2
Part (a) — find frequency:
Use v = f × λ, rearrange to f = v ÷ λ
f = 12 ÷ 4 = 3 Hz
3
Part (b) — find period:
T = 1 ÷ f = 1 ÷ 3 = 0.33 s
✏️ Practice 1 — Try it yourself!
A sound wave travels at 340 m/s and has a wavelength of 0.5 m.
(a) Find the frequency of the wave. (2 marks)
(b) Find the period. (2 marks)
Working space
(a) f = v ÷ λ = 340 ÷ 0.5 = 680 Hz
(b) T = 1 ÷ f = 1 ÷ 680 = 0.00147 s (= 1.47 × 10⁻³ s)
EXAMPLE 2
Finding wavelength when medium changes
Ch.10 Medium Change
A sound wave in air has frequency 500 Hz and speed 340 m/s. The wave enters water where its speed increases to 1500 m/s. The frequency does NOT change.
(a) Find the wavelength in air.  (b) Find the wavelength in water.
✏️ SOLUTION — Step by Step
1
Part (a) — wavelength in air:
λ = v ÷ f = 340 ÷ 500 = 0.68 m
2
Part (b) — wavelength in water:
Frequency stays the same (500 Hz), but speed changes.
λ = v ÷ f = 1500 ÷ 500 = 3 m
3
Key rule to remember: When a wave enters a new medium, frequency stays the same. Speed and wavelength change together (both increase or both decrease).
✏️ Practice 2 — Try it yourself!
A light wave in a vacuum has frequency 6 × 10¹⁴ Hz and speed 3 × 10⁸ m/s. It enters glass where its speed drops to 2 × 10⁸ m/s. Frequency does NOT change.
(a) Find the wavelength in vacuum. (2 marks)
(b) Find the wavelength in glass. (2 marks)
Working space
(a) λ = v ÷ f = (3×10⁸) ÷ (6×10¹⁴) = 5 × 10⁻⁷ m (= 500 nm, visible light)
(b) λ = v ÷ f = (2×10⁸) ÷ (6×10¹⁴) = 3.33 × 10⁻⁷ m
Notice: speed decreased → wavelength decreased too.
EXAMPLE 3
Reading a wave diagram — find amplitude, wavelength, frequency
Ch.10 Diagram
The wave diagram below shows a wave. The wave speed is 6 m/s.
Find: (a) amplitude   (b) wavelength   (c) frequency
✏️ SOLUTION — Step by Step
1
Part (a) — Amplitude:
From rest position (0) to crest = 4 m. A = 4 m
NOT 8 m — that would be crest to trough (= 2A)
2
Part (b) — Wavelength:
Crest to crest = from x = 2 m to x = 6 m... wait, let's read from start.
The wave repeats every 4 m (crest at x=2, next crest at x=6). Actually from the graph, one full cycle takes 4 m (x=0 to x=4). λ = 4 m
3
Part (c) — Frequency:
f = v ÷ λ = 6 ÷ 4 = 1.5 Hz
✏️ Practice 3 — Try it yourself!
A wave diagram shows: amplitude = 3 m, wavelength = 5 m. The wave speed is 20 m/s.
(a) Find the frequency. (2 marks)
(b) Find the period. (2 marks)
(c) If the amplitude doubles, what happens to the wave speed? (1 mark)
Working space
(a) f = v ÷ λ = 20 ÷ 5 = 4 Hz
(b) T = 1 ÷ f = 1 ÷ 4 = 0.25 s
(c) Wave speed does NOT change. Speed is determined by the medium, not the amplitude. Greater amplitude only means more energy.

Chapter 11 — The Electromagnetic Spectrum
All 7 types · Order by frequency · Properties · Uses · Dangers · EM wave calculations
📖 Concept 3 — The 7 Types of EM Waves (in order)
🧠 Mnemonic: Rather Mighty Intelligent Very Useful X-tra Great
(Radio · Microwave · Infrared · Visible · UV · X-rays · Gamma)
📖 Concept 4 — Properties ALL EM Waves Share
✅ Travel at 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum (speed of light)
✅ Are transverse waves
✅ Can travel through vacuum (no medium needed)
✅ Transfer energy
✅ Obey wave equation: c = f × λ
❌ Sound is NOT an EM wave — it is longitudinal and needs a medium to travel.
📖 Concept 5 — Uses & Dangers of Each EM Wave
WaveUsesDangers
Radio TV / radio broadcasting, Wi-Fi Low risk at normal intensity
Microwave Cooking food, mobile phones, satellite communication Heats internal body tissues (if absorbed)
Infrared TV remote controls, thermal imaging cameras, heat lamps Skin burns
Visible light Human vision, photography, fibre optics Very intense light can damage eyes
Ultraviolet (UV) Sterilisation, detecting forged banknotes, sunbeds Skin cancer, cataracts (eye damage), sunburn
X-rays Medical imaging (bones), airport security scanners Ionising — cell & DNA damage, cancer risk. Lead aprons used.
Gamma (γ) Cancer radiotherapy, sterilising medical/food equipment Most penetrating — ionising, DNA damage, cancer at high doses
⚠️ X-rays and Gamma rays are both ionising — they can knock electrons out of atoms, damaging DNA. This is why they're dangerous AND useful for killing cancer cells.
EXAMPLE 4
EM wave calculation using c = fλ
Ch.11 Calculation
A radio wave has frequency 90 MHz (= 9 × 10⁷ Hz). Speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
(a) Calculate its wavelength.  (b) Is this wavelength longer or shorter than a UV wave?
✏️ SOLUTION — Step by Step
1
Write what you know:
c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s  ·  f = 9 × 10⁷ Hz  ·  λ = ?
2
Part (a) — find wavelength:
Use c = f × λ, rearrange: λ = c ÷ f
λ = (3 × 10⁸) ÷ (9 × 10⁷) = 3.33 m
3
Part (b) — compare to UV:
Radio waves have lower frequency than UV → longer wavelength.
3.33 m (radio) is much longer than UV (~10⁻⁷ m). Radio wavelength is longer than UV.
✏️ Practice 4 — Try it yourself!
A gamma ray has frequency 3 × 10²⁰ Hz. Speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
(a) Calculate its wavelength. (2 marks)
(b) A microwave has wavelength 0.12 m. Calculate its frequency. (2 marks)
(c) Which has higher frequency — the gamma ray or the microwave? (1 mark)
Working space
(a) λ = c ÷ f = (3×10⁸) ÷ (3×10²⁰) = 1 × 10⁻¹² m (very short!)
(b) f = c ÷ λ = (3×10⁸) ÷ 0.12 = 2.5 × 10⁹ Hz
(c) Gamma ray has higher frequency (3×10²⁰ Hz >> 2.5×10⁹ Hz). Gamma is always the highest frequency EM wave.
EXAMPLE 5
Identify EM wave from use / danger — exam favourite!
Ch.11 Application
For each situation below, name the type of EM wave being used and explain why.
(a) A doctor takes an image of a patient's broken bone.
(b) A security guard checks a person's passport using a special lamp that makes hidden patterns appear.
(c) You press a button and your TV changes channel.
✏️ SOLUTION
a
X-rays — X-rays penetrate soft tissue but are absorbed by dense bone, creating a shadow image on film/detector. They are ionising, so only used when medically necessary.
b
Ultraviolet (UV) — Forged banknotes and documents use UV-reactive inks that glow under UV light. The hidden security pattern becomes visible.
c
Infrared — TV remote controls emit infrared pulses that carry coded signals to the TV receiver.
✏️ Practice 5 — Try it yourself!
Name the EM wave and give one reason for each:
(a) Used to cook food in an oven by making water molecules vibrate. (2 marks)
(b) Emitted by all warm objects; used in night-vision cameras. (2 marks)
(c) Used to kill cancer tumours inside the body; also used to sterilise surgical instruments. (2 marks)
(d) Causes sunburn and skin cancer; also used for sterilising water. (2 marks)
Working space
(a) Microwaves — they are absorbed by water molecules, causing them to vibrate and heat the food from within.
(b) Infrared — all objects above absolute zero emit infrared; night-vision cameras detect this heat signature.
(c) Gamma rays — highly penetrating and ionising; can reach tumours deep in the body and destroy cancer cells. Also kills bacteria on equipment.
(d) Ultraviolet (UV) — UV energy damages DNA of bacteria/viruses (sterilisation). Also causes sunburn and can lead to skin cancer with prolonged exposure.
EXAMPLE 6
Combined question — EM properties + calculation
Ch.11 Extended
(a) State THREE properties that all EM waves have in common.
(b) Explain why X-rays and gamma rays are considered more dangerous than radio waves or infrared.
(c) An X-ray has wavelength 1 × 10⁻¹⁰ m. Calculate its frequency. (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)
✏️ SOLUTION
a
1. All travel at 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum (speed of light).
2. All are transverse waves.
3. All can travel through vacuum without a medium.
b
X-rays and gamma rays have very high frequency (and short wavelength), giving them enough energy to be ionising — they can knock electrons out of atoms, breaking chemical bonds and damaging DNA. This can cause mutations and cancer. Radio waves and infrared only cause heating effects, which are less damaging.
c
Rearrange c = f × λ: f = c ÷ λ = (3 × 10⁸) ÷ (1 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 3 × 10¹⁸ Hz
✏️ Practice 6 — Final Challenge (Mixed Ch.10 + Ch.11)
(a) A visible light wave has wavelength 6 × 10⁻⁷ m. Calculate its frequency. (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s) (2 marks)

(b) List the 7 types of EM wave in order from lowest to highest frequency. (3 marks)

(c) State whether each wave below is transverse or longitudinal, and give one example:
   (i) Sound    (ii) Light    (iii) A water wave (3 marks)

(d) A microwave oven uses waves of frequency 2.45 GHz (= 2.45 × 10⁹ Hz). Calculate the wavelength. (2 marks)
Working space
(a) f = c ÷ λ = (3×10⁸) ÷ (6×10⁻⁷) = 5 × 10¹⁴ Hz

(b) Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible light → Ultraviolet → X-rays → Gamma rays
(Mnemonic: Rather Mighty Intelligent Very Useful X-tra Great)

(c) (i) Sound → Longitudinal (compressions & rarefactions along direction of travel)
(ii) Light → Transverse (vibrations perpendicular to direction of travel)
(iii) Water wave → Transverse (water surface moves up and down as wave travels sideways)

(d) λ = c ÷ f = (3×10⁸) ÷ (2.45×10⁹) = 0.122 m ≈ 12 cm
