📝 Writing Ionic Formulae
  • Use the cross-charge method: swap charges as subscripts
  • e.g. Ca²⁺ + Cl⁻ → CaCl₂
  • e.g. Al³⁺ + O²⁻ → Al₂O₃
  • Always reduce to simplest ratio
  • Polyatomic ions need brackets: Ca(NO₃)₂
⚖️ Balancing Equations
  • Write the unbalanced equation first
  • Balance the most complex molecule first
  • Balance H₂O last (if present)
  • Check atom counts on both sides
  • Add state symbols: (s) (l) (g) (aq)
🔢 Mole Calculation Steps
  • Write the balanced equation
  • Identify the mole ratio
  • Calculate moles of known substance
  • Convert using mole ratio
  • Convert to required units (g / dm³)
🚦 Limiting Reagent
  • Calculate moles of each reactant
  • Divide each by its mole ratio coefficient
  • The smaller value = limiting reagent
  • Use limiting reagent to calculate product
⚠ Common Mistakes
  • Molar volume at RTP = 24 dm³/mol; at STP (0°C) = 22.4 dm³/mol
  • Concentration formula: volume must be in dm³ (1000 cm³ = 1 dm³)
  • Polyatomic ions need brackets in formulas: Ca(NO₃)₂ not CaNO₃₂
Ch 7

Ionic Bonding

⚡ How It Forms
  • Metal loses electrons → cation (+)
  • Non-metal gains electrons → anion (−)
  • Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions
  • Forms a giant ionic lattice
🏗️ Structure
  • Giant ionic lattice structure
  • Each ion surrounded by oppositely charged ions
  • e.g. NaCl: each Na⁺ surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ (co-ordination no. = 6)
  • No discrete molecules
🌡️ Melting Point
  • High melting and boiling points
  • Reason: strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions
  • Requires large amounts of energy to overcome
  • Higher charge → stronger attraction → higher melting point
  • e.g. CaO (Ca²⁺/O²⁻) has higher m.p. than NaCl (Na⁺/Cl⁻)
💡 Electrical Conductivity
  • Solid state: ❌ No — ions fixed, cannot move
  • Molten: ✅ Yes — mobile ions
  • Aqueous solution: ✅ Yes — dissociated ions
💧 Solubility
  • Most ionic compounds dissolve in water
  • Polar water molecules attract the ions
  • Cation → attracted to O end (δ⁻)
  • Anion → attracted to H end (δ⁺)
  • Insoluble in organic solvents (e.g. hexane)
💔 Brittleness
  • Ionic crystals are brittle
  • When force is applied, layers shift
  • Same-charge ions align → repel each other
  • Crystal cracks / shatters
  • (Contrast with metals: layers slide without repulsion)
⚠ Exam Traps
  • Conductivity requires mobile ions — solid ionic compounds cannot conduct
  • High melting point → use the phrase strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions
  • CaO has higher m.p. than NaCl because Ca²⁺/O²⁻ have higher charges → stronger forces
  • Ionic solids are brittle due to repulsion, NOT because bonds are weak

Covalent Bonding

Covalent substances fall into two very different categories:

PropertySimple MolecularGiant Covalent
Melting pointLow — weak intermolecular forcesVery high — many strong covalent bonds
Electrical conductivityDoes not conduct — no free chargesDoes not conduct (except graphite)
Solubility in waterMost are insoluble; polar molecules may dissolveInsoluble
ExamplesH₂O, CO₂, HCl, Br₂, I₂, OF₂, N₂H₄Diamond, Graphite, SiO₂, Cubic BN
💎 Diamond
  • Each C bonded to 4 other C atoms
  • 3D tetrahedral network
  • Very high melting point (~3550°C)
  • Does not conduct — no free electrons
  • Hardest natural substance
  • Insoluble in water AND organic solvents
✏️ Graphite
  • Each C bonded to 3 other C atoms
  • Layered structure; weak forces between layers
  • High melting point
  • Conducts electricity — delocalized electrons move within layers
  • Used as lubricant (layers can slide)
🔩 Cubic Boron Nitride (BN)
  • Diamond-like structure (see Quiz Q20)
  • B and N atoms alternate
  • Extremely hard (used as abrasive)
  • Does not conduct
  • Not brittle — brittleness is an ionic property
🟣 Iodine (I₂)
  • Simple molecular structure
  • Solid at room temperature (but low m.p.)
  • Insoluble in water
  • Soluble in organic solvents (e.g. hexane)
  • Does not conduct electricity
  • (Quiz Q10: option D "soluble in water and organic solvents" is WRONG)
⚠ Most Common Errors
  • Simple molecular low m.p. is due to weak intermolecular forces — NOT weak covalent bonds!
  • OF₂ is simple molecular, NOT giant covalent (Quiz Q16)
  • Only graphite conducts among giant covalent substances
  • Iodine is insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents
  • Diamond is insoluble in water AND organic solvents
Ch 9

Metallic Bonding

🔬 Structure
  • Positive metal ions arranged in a lattice
  • Surrounded by a sea of delocalized electrons
  • Strong electrostatic attraction between metal ions and electron sea
  • Orderly lattice arrangement
💡 Electrical Conductivity
  • Solid: ✅ Yes
  • Molten: ✅ Yes
  • Reason: delocalized electrons can move freely
  • (Key difference from ionic: metals conduct in solid state!)
🌡️ Melting Point
  • Generally high — strong metallic bonds
  • Exception: Mercury (Hg) is liquid at room temperature
  • Hg is still a metal (has delocalized electrons)
  • Hg has weaker metallic bonds than most metals
🔨 Malleability
  • Malleable and ductile
  • Layers of ions can slide over each other
  • Delocalized electrons redistribute to maintain bonding
  • Unlike ionic: sliding does NOT cause same-charge repulsion
⚠ Mercury (Hg) — Frequently Examined!
  • Mercury is the only liquid metal at room temperature and pressure
  • Mercury(II) ions + delocalized electrons = metallic bonding (NOT molecular)
  • Mercury is NOT composed of discrete molecules (Quiz Q2 option (2) is wrong)
  • Mercury still conducts electricity (delocalized electrons)
